Ecosystem Value and Public Benefit

This page explains why urban woodlands generate important benefits for people, neighbourhoods, and the wider environment. It focuses on the idea that many of these benefits are real and valuable, even when they are not clearly priced in markets.

Why this matters

Russia Dock Woodlands and Stave Hill Ecological Park are more than pleasant green spaces. They support biodiversity, learning, recreation, mental wellbeing, and wider environmental resilience. Yet many of these benefits remain invisible in budgets, planning decisions, and everyday management debates.

What are ecosystem services?

Ecosystem services are the benefits that people receive from nature. In an urban woodland, these benefits are not only ecological. They also include social, educational, and everyday quality-of-life benefits.

Provisioning

Natural systems can provide material resources such as water, biomass, and other ecological outputs.

Regulating

Woodlands and parks help regulate temperature, water, habitat quality, and other environmental processes.

Cultural

Urban nature supports recreation, identity, learning, community life, and mental wellbeing.

Supporting

Soils, habitats, species interactions, and nutrient cycles underpin the wider health of the ecosystem.

Why urban nature is often undervalued

Many ecosystem services function as public goods. People benefit from them collectively, but they are often under-recognised because they are not directly bought and sold. Some benefits, such as recreation, are visible and immediate. Others, such as climate regulation or ecological resilience, are less obvious and more easily ignored.

The key issue is visibility. Valuation does not create value from nowhere. It helps make already-existing ecological and social benefits more visible to planning, funding, and governance processes.

How people think about ecosystem value

The presentation outlined several different approaches to ecosystem service valuation. Rather than focusing on numerical estimates, this page highlights what each method helps us understand.

Hedonic pricing

Looks at how environmental quality may shape nearby property values, showing that access to green space can influence how places are valued.

Holistic valuation

Treats the park as natural capital and asks how ecological systems contribute to wider neighbourhood attractiveness, health, and livability.

Benefit transfer

Uses evidence from previous ecosystem studies to estimate likely value patterns for similar habitats and services.

Willingness to pay

Explores how strongly local people support the site and what kinds of practical improvements they care about most.

What this means in practice

For planning and policy

  • Makes hidden trade-offs easier to see
  • Strengthens arguments for conservation and long-term maintenance
  • Supports funding decisions beyond short-term visible fixes
  • Shows that nature should be treated as core infrastructure, not leftover land

For community wellbeing

  • Highlights the social value of everyday access to nature
  • Supports healthier, safer, and more attractive neighbourhoods
  • Strengthens community claims to protect shared green spaces
  • Links environmental quality with public wellbeing and inclusion

Key takeaway

The main message is simple: woodlands and ecological parks create value that is ecological, social, and civic at the same time. Ecosystem valuation helps translate that wider value into a language that can support better decisions, fairer funding, and stronger public stewardship.
