Governance and Shared Stewardship

This page explains a proposed governance model for the wider Rotherhithe ecological landscape. Rather than treating each site as isolated, the proposal imagines a more connected and participatory system that links ecology, community decision-making, and long-term stewardship.

Deliberative democracy Polycentric governance Community co-decision

Why governance matters here

Russia Dock Woodlands, Stave Hill, nearby green spaces, and community assets are ecologically connected, but governance is often fragmented. A more unified structure could help align ecological priorities, improve coordination across sites, and give local communities a stronger role in decisions that affect the landscape.

The governance idea in brief

The proposal is based on three linked ideas: nature as a shared commons, governance distributed across multiple actors rather than one single authority, and stronger community participation in decision-making. In the source presentation, this is framed through deliberative democracy, natural capital as a commons, and polycentric governance. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}

Natural capital as a commons

The landscape is treated as a shared ecological asset whose value is collective, long-term, and not reducible to one single owner or institution.

Polycentric governance

Decision-making is spread across connected actors and scales, rather than being controlled by one body alone.

Participatory governance

Local people are not only consulted, but given a more formal and meaningful role in shaping outcomes.

Current governance challenges

The presentation identifies a series of interconnected weaknesses in the existing model, including fragmented authority, defensive conservation, artificial hydrology, and weak financial and ecological autonomy under strong development pressure. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}

Fragmented authority

Different sites and actors operate separately, which makes joined-up ecological management harder.

Cross-site problems

Water, species, access, and visitor behaviour move across boundaries, but decisions often do not.

Development pressure

Rapid change in the surrounding area creates pressure without a matching long-term governance structure for the ecological landscape.

The proposed two-house model

A central feature of the proposal is a twin-house structure. One side focuses on ecological and technical stewardship, while the other focuses on community priorities, participation, and co-decision. The original deck refers to these as the Ecology House and the Community House. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}

Ecology House

  • Coordinates ecological priorities across sites
  • Supports habitat strategy, monitoring, and technical standards
  • Links site-level action to borough and metropolitan agendas
  • Provides a unified ecological voice in negotiations

Community House

  • Brings together residents, Friends groups, and community organisations
  • Moves beyond consultation toward co-decision
  • Supports participation in social programmes, events, and local priorities
  • Creates a formal route for community influence over change

How the structure works

In the proposal, the two houses sit within a wider landscape partnership. Site-level management continues, but a landscape-scale layer is added above it to coordinate strategy, funding priorities, and larger cross-site decisions. The uploaded slides show this as an overarching Rotherhithe eco-landscape partnership structure. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

In practice, this means: day-to-day management stays local, but bigger questions — such as land use, funding bids, ecological connectivity, and shared priorities — are handled through a more joined-up system with both ecological and community representation.

Implementation pathway

The source presentation proposes a 12–18 month pilot rather than an immediate permanent restructuring. The aim is to test coordination, define shared priorities, deliver a small number of visible projects, and then review what works. :contentReference[oaicite:5]{index=5}

Months 1–2

Set up the pilot partnership

Bring together key stakeholders and establish a clearer landscape-level coordination structure.

Months 2–4

Agree priorities and funding routes

Identify a shortlist of joint priorities and align them with funding opportunities and wider development contributions.

Months 4–12

Deliver visible “seed” projects

Test small but visible cross-site actions that demonstrate the value of coordinated ecological and community governance.

Months 12–18

Review and adapt

Assess what changed, what worked well, and what should be improved in the next cycle.

Why this matters for residents

The proposal is framed not as abstract institutional design, but as a way to protect and improve the woods, ponds, gardens, and community spaces that people already value. It also seeks to ensure that change linked to Canada Water and surrounding development is matched by stronger ecological stewardship and fairer community influence. :contentReference[oaicite:6]{index=6}

Key takeaway: this model is about moving from fragmented management and consultation-based participation towards a more coordinated, landscape-scale, and genuinely participatory form of environmental governance.
